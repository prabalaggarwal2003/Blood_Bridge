Blood Compatibility

Blood compatibility, also known as blood transfusion compatibility, refers to the compatibility between the blood of a donor and the blood of a recipient. It's crucial for blood transfusions to avoid adverse reactions, such as immune system responses that could be life-threatening. The compatibility depends on the presence or absence of specific antigens and antibodies in the blood.

Types of Blood Groups

There are four main blood groups determined by the presence or absence of two antigens, A and B, on the surface of red blood cells:

    Blood Group A

    : Has A antigens on the surface of its red blood cells and B antibodies in the plasma.

    Blood Group B

    : Has B antigens on the surface of its red blood cells and A antibodies in the plasma.

    Blood Group AB

    : Has both A and B antigens on the surface of its red blood cells and no A or B antibodies in the plasma.

    Blood Group O

    : Has no A or B antigens on the surface of its red blood cells and both A and B antibodies in the plasma.

Rh Factor Compatibility

Rh-positive individuals can receive blood from Rh-positive and Rh-negative donors.

Rh-negative individuals should only receive blood from Rh-negative donors to prevent Rh incompatibility reactions.
